<p><b>Jiang Zemin</b> (<span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a>: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="/ŋ/: &#39;ng&#39; in &#39;sing&#39;">ŋ</span></span> <wbr /><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ʒ/: &#39;s&#39; in &#39;pleasure&#39;">ʒ</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <span class="nowrap"><small><a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">US</a>: </small><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span><span title="/ŋ/: &#39;ng&#39; in &#39;sing&#39;">ŋ</span></span> <wbr /><span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> born 17 August 1926)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> is a retired Chinese politician who served as <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary of the Communist Party of China</a> from 1989 to 2002, as <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Central_Military_Commission" title="Chairman of the Central Military Commission">Chairman of the Central Military Commission</a> from 1989 to 2004, and as <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="President of the People&#39;s Republic of China">President of the People's Republic of China</a> from 1993 to 2003. Jiang has been described as the "<a href="/wiki/Leadership_core" title="Leadership core">core</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Generations_of_Chinese_leadership" title="Generations of Chinese leadership">third generation</a>" of <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party</a> leaders since 1989.
</p><p>Jiang Zemin came to power unexpectedly as a 'compromise candidate' following the <a href="/wiki/Tiananmen_Square_protests_of_1989" title="Tiananmen Square protests of 1989">Tiananmen Square protests of 1989</a>, when he replaced <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Ziyang" title="Zhao Ziyang">Zhao Ziyang</a> as <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary</a> after Zhao was ousted for his support for the student movement. With the waning influence of <a href="/wiki/Eight_Elders" title="Eight Elders">Eight Elders</a> due to old age and with the death of <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a>, Jiang consolidated his hold on power and became the "<a href="/wiki/Paramount_leader" title="Paramount leader">paramount leader</a>" of the country in the 1990s.
</p><p>Under Jiang's leadership, China experienced substantial economic growth with the continuation of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_economic_reform" title="Chinese economic reform">reforms</a>, saw the peaceful return of <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_sovereignty_over_Hong_Kong" class="mw-redirect" title="Transfer of sovereignty over Hong Kong">Hong Kong from the United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_sovereignty_over_Macau" title="Transfer of sovereignty over Macau">Macau from Portugal</a>, and improved its relations with the outside world while the Communist Party maintained its tight control over the government. His contributions to party doctrine, known as the "<a href="/wiki/Three_Represents" title="Three Represents">Three Represents</a>," were written into the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Constitution of the Communist Party of China">party's constitution</a> in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Jiang vacated the post of party General Secretary and <a href="/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Politburo Standing Committee of the Communist Party of China">Politburo Standing Committee</a> in 2002, but did not relinquish all of his leadership titles until 2005, and continued to influence affairs until much later. At the age of 92&#160;years, 60&#160;days, Jiang Zemin is the longest-living <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary</a> and <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="President of the People&#39;s Republic of China">President of the People's Republic of China</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_ascendancy">Background and ascendancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and ascendancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jiang Zemin was born in the city of <a href="/wiki/Yangzhou" title="Yangzhou">Yangzhou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangsu" title="Jiangsu">Jiangsu</a>. His ancestral home was Jiangwan (<span lang="zh-Hans" title="Chinese language text">江湾</span>) in <a href="/wiki/Wuyuan_County,_Jiangxi" title="Wuyuan County, Jiangxi">Wuyuan County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangxi" title="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</a>. This was also the hometown of a number of prominent figures in Chinese academic and intellectual establishments. Jiang grew up during the years of <a href="/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War" title="Second Sino-Japanese War">Japanese occupation</a>. His uncle, also his foster father, Jiang Shangqing, died fighting the Japanese in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a><sup id="cite_ref-Epoch_Times_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epoch_Times-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and is considered in Jiang Zemin's time to be a national hero.<sup id="cite_ref-NTD_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTD-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Since Shangqing had no heirs, Shangqing's elder brother, Jiang's biological father Jiang Shijun, the vice officer of the publicity department of Japanese-ruled Nanking government, let Jiang become the adopted son of Shangqing's wife, his aunt, Wang Zhelan, whom he referred to as "Niang" (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">娘</span>; literally&#58; "Mom").<sup id="cite_ref-Epoch_Times_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epoch_Times-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There is some doubt if Jiang was really adopted at the time:
</p>
<ul><li>Shangqing's daughter Zehui once said her family was too poor to have enough food after his death, which indicated that Jiang Zemin’s father, despite his wealth and power, never supported Shangqing’s family</li>
<li>it was unlikely that Jiang Zemin’s father would have allowed Jiang Shangqing to adopt Zemin, because per Chinese traditions, Zemin, as an eldest son, could not be adopted<sup id="cite_ref-Epoch_Times_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Epoch_Times-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NTD_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NTD-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JZM1947.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/JZM1947.jpg/130px-JZM1947.jpg" width="130" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/JZM1947.jpg/195px-JZM1947.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/JZM1947.jpg/260px-JZM1947.jpg 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JZM1947.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Graduation photo of Jiang Zemin, taken in 1947.</div></div></div>
<p>Jiang attended the Department of Electrical Engineering at the <a href="/wiki/National_Central_University" title="National Central University">National Central University</a> in Japanese-occupied <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a> before being transferred to <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an_Jiaotong_University" title="Xi&#39;an Jiaotong University">National Chiao Tung University</a>. He graduated there in 1947 with a bachelor's degree in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>.
</p><p>He claims that he joined the Communist Party of China when he was in college.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> After the establishment of the People's Republic of China, Jiang received his training at the <a href="/wiki/Zavod_Imeni_Likhacheva" class="mw-redirect" title="Zavod Imeni Likhacheva">Stalin Automobile Works</a> in <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> in the 1950s. He also worked for Changchun's <a href="/wiki/First_Automobile_Works" class="mw-redirect" title="First Automobile Works">First Automobile Works</a>. He was eventually transferred to government services, where he began to rise in prominence and rank, eventually becoming a member of the Central Committee of the Communist Party, Minister of Electronic Industries in 1983.
</p><p>In 1985 he became Mayor of Shanghai, and subsequently the <a href="/wiki/Party_chief_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Party chief of the Communist Party of China">Party Secretary of Shanghai</a>. Jiang received mixed reviews as mayor. Many of his critics dismissed him as a "flower pot", a Chinese term for someone who only seems useful, but actually gets nothing done.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC:_Profile:_Jiang_Zemin_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC:_Profile:_Jiang_Zemin-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Many credited Shanghai's growth during the period to <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Rongji" title="Zhu Rongji">Zhu Rongji</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Jiang was an ardent believer, during this period, in <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping" title="Deng Xiaoping">Deng Xiaoping</a>'s economic reforms. In an attempt to curb student discontent in 1986, Jiang recited the <a href="/wiki/Gettysburg_Address" title="Gettysburg Address">Gettysburg Address</a> in English in front of a group of student protesters.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:147px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JZM1962.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/JZM1962.jpg/145px-JZM1962.jpg" width="145" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/JZM1962.jpg/218px-JZM1962.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/JZM1962.jpg/290px-JZM1962.jpg 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="523" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JZM1962.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jiang Zemin in 1962</div></div></div>
<p>Jiang was described as having a passable command of several foreign languages,<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2001_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2001-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>. One of his favorite activities was to engage foreign visitors in small talk on arts and literature in their native language, in addition to singing foreign songs in the original language.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2001_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2001-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He became friends with <a href="/wiki/Allen_Broussard" title="Allen Broussard">Allen Broussard</a>, the African-American judge who visited Shanghai in 1987 and Brazilian actress <a href="/wiki/Luc%C3%A9lia_Santos" title="Lucélia Santos">Lucélia Santos</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Jiang was elevated to national politics in 1987, automatically becoming a member of the Politburo of the <a href="/wiki/CPC_Central_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="CPC Central Committee">CPC Central Committee</a> because it is customarily dictated that the Party Secretary of Shanghai would also have a seat in the Politburo. In 1989, China was in crisis over the <a href="/wiki/Tiananmen_Square_protests_of_1989" title="Tiananmen Square protests of 1989">Tiananmen Square protest</a>, and the central government was in conflict on how to handle the protesters. In June, Deng Xiaoping dismissed liberal <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Ziyang" title="Zhao Ziyang">Zhao Ziyang</a>, who was considered to be too conciliatory toward the student protestors. At the time, Jiang was the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Shanghai" title="Politics of Shanghai">Shanghai Party secretary</a>, the top figure in China's new economic center. In an incident with the <i><a href="/wiki/World_Economic_Herald" title="World Economic Herald">World Economic Herald</a></i>, Jiang closed down the newspaper, deeming it to be harmful. The handling of the crisis in Shanghai was noticed by Beijing, and then by paramount leader Deng Xiaoping. As the protests escalated and then Party general secretary <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Ziyang" title="Zhao Ziyang">Zhao Ziyang</a> was removed from office, Jiang was selected by the Party leaders as a compromise candidate over <a href="/wiki/Tianjin" title="Tianjin">Tianjin</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Li_Ruihuan" title="Li Ruihuan">Li Ruihuan</a>, Premier <a href="/wiki/Li_Peng" title="Li Peng">Li Peng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Li_Xiannian" title="Li Xiannian">Li Xiannian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chen_Yun" title="Chen Yun">Chen Yun</a>, and the retired elders to become the new General Secretary. Before that, he had been considered to be an unlikely candidate.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Within three years, Deng had transferred most power in the state, party and military to Jiang.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_personal_life">Family and personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family and personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jiang married <a href="/wiki/Wang_Yeping" title="Wang Yeping">Wang Yeping</a> in 1949, also a native of Yangzhou.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> She graduated from <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_International_Studies_University" title="Shanghai International Studies University">Shanghai International Studies University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pd_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pd-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pd_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pd-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> They had two sons together, <a href="/wiki/Jiang_Mianheng" title="Jiang Mianheng">Jiang Mianheng</a> born in 1951 and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Miankang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jiang Miankang (page does not exist)">Jiang Miankang</a> born in 1956. Jiang Mengheng went on to be a successful academic and businessman, working within the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_space_program" title="Chinese space program">Chinese space program</a>, and founded Grace Semiconductor Manufacturing Corporation.
</p><p>Jiang Zemin has had a long-running friendship with the singer <a href="/wiki/Song_Zuying" title="Song Zuying">Song Zuying</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Following the rise of <a href="/wiki/Xi_jinping" class="mw-redirect" title="Xi jinping">Xi jinping</a>, Song  and other Jiang loyalists, including her brother Song Zuyu, fell under investigation for corruption.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_leadership">Early leadership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early leadership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jiang was elevated to the country's top job in 1989 with a fairly small power base inside the party, and thus, very little actual power.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> His most reliable allies were the powerful party elders – <a href="/wiki/Chen_Yun" title="Chen Yun">Chen Yun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Li_Xiannian" title="Li Xiannian">Li Xiannian</a>. He was believed to be simply a transitional figure until a more stable successor government to Deng could be put in place. Other prominent Party and military figures like <a href="/wiki/Yang_Shangkun" title="Yang Shangkun">Yang Shangkun</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Yang_Baibing" title="Yang Baibing">Yang Baibing</a> were believed to be planning a coup. Jiang used Deng Xiaoping as a back-up to his leadership in the first few years. Jiang, who was believed<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> to have a neo-conservative slant, warned against "bourgeois liberalization". Deng's belief, however, stipulated that the only solution to keeping the legitimacy of Communist rule over China was to continue the drive for modernization and economic reform, and therefore placed himself at odds with Jiang.
</p><p>At the first meeting of the new <a href="/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Politburo Standing Committee of the Communist Party of China">Politburo Standing Committee</a>, after the Tiananmen Square massacre of 1989, Jiang criticized the previous period as "hard on the economy, soft on politics" and advocated increasing political thought work.<sup id="cite_ref-brady08_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brady08-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Anne-Marie_Brady" title="Anne-Marie Brady">Anne-Marie Brady</a> wrote that "Jiang Zemin was a long time political cadre with a nose for ideological work and its importance. This meeting marked the beginning of a new era in propaganda and political thought work in China." Soon after, the Central Propaganda Department was given more resources and power, "including the power to go in to the propaganda-related work units and cleanse the ranks of those who had been supportive of the democracy movement."<sup id="cite_ref-brady08_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brady08-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Deng grew critical of Jiang's leadership in 1992. During Deng's southern tours, he subtly suggested that the pace of reform was not fast enough, and the "central leadership" (i.e. Jiang) had most responsibility. Jiang grew ever more cautious, and rallied behind Deng's reforms completely. In 1993, Jiang coined the new "<a href="/wiki/Socialist_market_economy" title="Socialist market economy">socialist market economy</a>" to move China's centrally-planned socialist economy into essentially a government-regulated capitalist <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">market economy</a>. It was a huge step to take in the realization of Deng's "Socialism with Chinese characteristics". At the same time, Jiang elevated many of his supporters from Shanghai to high government positions, after regaining Deng's confidence. He abolished the outdated <a href="/wiki/Central_Advisory_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Advisory Committee">Central Advisory Committee</a>, an advisory body composed of revolutionary party elders. He became <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary of the Communist Party of China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Central_Military_Commission" title="Chairman of the Central Military Commission">Chairman of the Central Military Commission</a> in 1989, followed by his election to the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="President of the People&#39;s Republic of China">Presidency</a> in March 1993.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leader_of_Communist_China">Leader of Communist China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Leader of Communist China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the early 1990s, post-Tiananmen economic reforms had stabilized and the country was on a consistent growth trajectory. At the same time, China faced a myriad of economic and social problems. At Deng's state funeral in 1997, Jiang delivered the elder statesman's eulogy. Jiang had inherited a China rampant with political corruption, and regional economies growing too rapidly for the stability of the entire country. Deng's policy that "some areas can get rich before others" led to an opening wealth gap between coastal regions and the interior provinces. The unprecedented economic growth and the deregulation in a number of heavy industries led to the closing of many <a href="/wiki/State-owned_enterprise" title="State-owned enterprise">state-owned enterprises</a> (SOEs), breaking the <a href="/wiki/Iron_rice_bowl" title="Iron rice bowl">iron rice bowl</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As a result, unemployment rates skyrocketed, rising as high as 40% in some urban areas. <a href="/wiki/Stock_market" title="Stock market">Stock markets</a> fluctuated greatly. The scale of rural migration into urban areas was unprecedented anywhere, and little was being done to address an ever-increasing urban-rural wealth gap. Official reports put the figure on the percentage of China's GDP being moved and abused by corrupt officials at 10%.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A chaotic environment of illegal bonds issued from civil and military officials resulted in much of the corrupted wealth ending up in foreign countries. The re-emergence of <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime_in_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Organized crime in China">organized crime</a> and a surge in crime rates began to plague cities. A careless stance on the destruction of the environment furthered concerns voiced by intellectuals.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Jiang's biggest aim in the economy was stability, and he believed that a stable government with highly centralised power would be a prerequisite, choosing to postpone political reform, which in many facets of governance exacerbated the ongoing problems.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Jiang continued pouring funds to develop the <a href="/wiki/Special_Economic_Zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Economic Zone">Special Economic Zones</a> and coastal regions. Beginning in 1996, Jiang began a series of reforms in the state-controlled media aimed at promoting the "core of leadership" under himself, and at the same time crushing some of his political opponents. The personality enhancements in the media were largely frowned upon during the Deng era, and had not been seen since the Mao era in the late 1970s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Daily" title="People&#39;s Daily">People's Daily</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/China_Central_Television" title="China Central Television">CCTV</a>-1's 7 pm <i><a href="/wiki/Xinwen_Lianbo" title="Xinwen Lianbo">Xinwen Lianbo</a></i> each had Jiang-related events as the front-page or top stories, a fact that remained until Hu Jintao's media administrative changes in 2006. Jiang appeared casual in front of Western media, and gave an unprecedented interview with <a href="/wiki/Mike_Wallace" title="Mike Wallace">Mike Wallace</a> of CBS in 2000 at <a href="/wiki/Beidaihe" class="mw-redirect" title="Beidaihe">Beidaihe</a>. He would often use foreign languages in front of the camera, albeit not always fluently. In an encounter with a Hong Kong reporter in 2000 regarding the central government's apparent "imperial order" of supporting <a href="/wiki/Tung_Chee-hwa" title="Tung Chee-hwa">Tung Chee-hwa</a> to seek a second term as <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chief Executive of Hong Kong">Chief Executive of Hong Kong</a>, Jiang scolded the Hong Kong journalists as "too simple, sometimes naive" in English.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The event was shown on Hong Kong television that night.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expulsion_of_Falun_Gong">Expulsion of Falun Gong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Expulsion of Falun Gong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In June 1999, Jiang established an extralegal department, the <a href="/wiki/610_Office" title="610 Office">6-10 Office</a>, to expel Falun Gong from mainland China.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> On 20 July, security forces abducted and detained thousands of Falun Gong organizers they identified as leaders.<sup id="cite_ref-Tong_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tong-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The persecution that followed was characterized a nationwide campaign of propaganda, as well as the large-scale arbitrary imprisonment and coercive reeducation of Falun Gong organizers, sometimes resulting in death.<sup id="cite_ref-HRW_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HRW-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zeminhandshake.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Zeminhandshake.JPG/220px-Zeminhandshake.JPG" width="220" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Zeminhandshake.JPG 1.5x" data-file-width="240" data-file-height="219" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zeminhandshake.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jiang Zemin with <a href="/wiki/US_President" class="mw-redirect" title="US President">US President</a> <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> in 1999.</div></div></div>
<p>Jiang went on a groundbreaking <a href="/wiki/State_visit" title="State visit">state visit</a> to the United States in 1997, drawing various crowds in protest from the Tibet Independence Movement to supporters of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_democracy_movement" title="Chinese democracy movement">Chinese democracy movement</a>. He made a speech at Harvard University, part of it in English, but could not escape questions on democracy and freedom. In the official summit meeting with US President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>, the tone was relaxed as Jiang and Clinton sought common ground while largely ignoring areas of disagreement. Clinton would visit China in June 1998, and vowed that China and the United States were partners in the world, and not adversaries. When American-led <a href="/wiki/NATO_Bombing_of_the_Chinese_embassy_in_Belgrade" class="mw-redirect" title="NATO Bombing of the Chinese embassy in Belgrade">NATO bombed the Chinese Embassy in Belgrade</a> in 1999, Jiang seemed to have put up a harsh stance for show at home, but in reality only performed symbolic gestures of protest, and no solid action.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Jiang's foreign policy was for the most part passive and non-confrontational. A personal friend of former Canadian Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jean_Chr%C3%A9tien" title="Jean Chrétien">Jean Chrétien</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  Jiang strengthened China's economic stature abroad, attempting to establish cordial relations with countries whose trade is largely confined to the American economic sphere. Despite this, there were at least three serious flare-ups between China and the US during Jiang's tenure: the <a href="/wiki/Third_Taiwan_Strait_Crisis" title="Third Taiwan Strait Crisis">Third Taiwan Strait Crisis</a> in 1996, the above-mentioned NATO bombing of Serbia, and the <a href="/wiki/Hainan_Island_incident" title="Hainan Island incident">Hainan Island incident</a> in April 2001.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_development">Economic development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economic development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jiang did not specialize in economics, and in 1997 handed most of the economic governance of the country to <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Rongji" title="Zhu Rongji">Zhu Rongji</a>, who became <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_State_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Premier of the State Council">Premier</a>, and remained in office through the <a href="/wiki/Asian_financial_crisis" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian financial crisis">Asian financial crisis</a>. Under their joint leadership, <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a> has sustained an average of 8% GDP growth annually, achieving the highest rate of per capita economic growth in major world economies, raising eyebrows around the world with its astonishing speed. This was mostly achieved by continuing the process of a transition to a market economy. Strong party control over China was cemented by the PRC's successful bid to join the <a href="/wiki/World_Trade_Organization" title="World Trade Organization">World Trade Organization</a> and Beijing winning the bid to host the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Summer Olympics</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entrenching_Three_Represents">Entrenching Three Represents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Entrenching Three Represents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ideology_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Ideology of the Communist Party of China">Ideology of the Communist Party of China</a></div>
<p>Before he transferred power to a younger generation of leaders, Jiang had his theory of <a href="/wiki/Three_Represents" title="Three Represents">Three Represents</a> written into the Party's constitution, alongside <a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxism–Leninism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maoism" title="Maoism">Mao Zedong Thought</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping_Theory" title="Deng Xiaoping Theory">Deng Xiaoping Theory</a> at the 16th CPC Congress in 2002. Critics believe this is just another piece added to Jiang's <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">cult of personality</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Who? (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> others have seen practical applications of the theory as a guiding ideology in the future direction of the CPC. Largely speculated to step down from all positions by international media, rival <a href="/wiki/Li_Ruihuan" title="Li Ruihuan">Li Ruihuan</a>'s resignation in 2002 prompted analysts to rethink the man. The theory of Three Represents was believed by many political analysts to be Jiang's effort at extending his vision to Marxist–Leninist principles, and therefore elevating himself alongside previous Chinese Marxist philosophers Mao and Deng.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gradual_retirement">Gradual retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gradual retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Shanghai_clique" title="Shanghai clique">Shanghai clique</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jiang_zemin_with_wife_bush_with_laura.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Jiang_zemin_with_wife_bush_with_laura.jpg/220px-Jiang_zemin_with_wife_bush_with_laura.jpg" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Jiang_zemin_with_wife_bush_with_laura.jpg/330px-Jiang_zemin_with_wife_bush_with_laura.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Jiang_zemin_with_wife_bush_with_laura.jpg/440px-Jiang_zemin_with_wife_bush_with_laura.jpg 2x" data-file-width="514" data-file-height="334" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jiang_zemin_with_wife_bush_with_laura.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jiang Zemin with wife and <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> with wife in <a href="/wiki/Crawford,_Texas" title="Crawford, Texas">Crawford, Texas</a>, 25 October 2002.</div></div></div>
<p>In 2002, Jiang stepped down from the powerful <a href="/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Politburo Standing Committee">Politburo Standing Committee</a> and as General Secretary to make way for a "fourth generation" of leadership headed by <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jintao" title="Hu Jintao">Hu Jintao</a>, beginning a transition of power that would last several years. Hu assumed Jiang's title as party head, becoming the new <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary of the Communist Party of China</a>. At the 16th Party Congress held in the autumn of 2002, observers noted at the time that six out of the nine new members of Standing Committee were considered part of Jiang's so-called "<a href="/wiki/Shanghai_Clique" class="mw-redirect" title="Shanghai Clique">Shanghai Clique</a>", the most prominent being Vice President <a href="/wiki/Zeng_Qinghong" title="Zeng Qinghong">Zeng Qinghong</a>, who had served as Jiang's chief of staff for many years, and Vice Premier <a href="/wiki/Huang_Ju" title="Huang Ju">Huang Ju</a>, a former party chief of <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a>.
</p><p>Although Jiang retained the chairmanship of the powerful <a href="/wiki/Central_Military_Commission" title="Central Military Commission">Central Military Commission</a>, most members of the commission were professional military men. <i>Liberation Army Daily</i>, a publication thought to represent the views of the CMC majority, printed an article on 11 March 2003 which quotes two army delegates as saying, "Having one center is called 'loyalty', while having two centers will result in 'problems.'"<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> This was widely interpreted as a criticism of Jiang's attempt to exercise dual leadership with Hu on the model of Deng Xiaoping.
</p><p>Hu succeeded Jiang as party leader in November 2002. To the surprise of many observers, evidence of Jiang's continuing influence on public policy abruptly disappeared from the official media. Jiang was conspicuously silent during the <a href="/wiki/SARS" class="mw-redirect" title="SARS">SARS</a> crisis, especially when compared to the very public profile of Hu and the newly anointed Premier, <a href="/wiki/Wen_Jiabao" title="Wen Jiabao">Wen Jiabao</a>. It has been argued that the institutional arrangements created by the 16th Congress have left Jiang in a position where he cannot exercise much influence.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Although many of the members of the Politburo Standing Committee were associated with him, the Standing Committee does not necessarily have command authority over the civilian bureaucracy.
</p><p>On 19 September 2004, after a four-day meeting of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Committee_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="Central Committee of the Communist Party of China">Central Committee</a>, Jiang relinquished his post as chairman of the party's Central Military Commission, his last post in the party. Six months later he resigned his last significant post, chairman of the Central Military Commission of the state.  This followed weeks of speculation that forces inside the party were pressing Jiang to step aside. Jiang's term was supposed to have lasted until 2007. Hu also succeeded Jiang as the CMC chairman, but, in an apparent political defeat for Jiang, General <a href="/wiki/Xu_Caihou" title="Xu Caihou">Xu Caihou</a>, and not Zeng Qinghong was appointed to succeed Hu as vice chairman, as was initially speculated. This power transition formally marked the end of Jiang's era in China, which roughly lasted from 1993 to 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_appearances_after_retirement">Official appearances after retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Official appearances after retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Jiang continued to make official appearances after giving up his last title in 2004.  In China's <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_precedence_in_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Orders of precedence in the People&#39;s Republic of China">strictly defined protocol sequence</a>, Jiang's name always appeared immediately after Hu Jintao's and in front of the remaining sitting members of the <a href="/wiki/Politburo_Standing_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Politburo Standing Committee">Politburo Standing Committee</a>.  In 2007, Jiang was seen with Hu Jintao on stage at a ceremony celebrating the 80th anniversary of the founding of the People's Liberation Army,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and toured the Military Museum of the Chinese People's Revolution with Li Peng, Zhu Rongji, and other former senior officials.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> On 8 August 2008, Jiang appeared at the opening ceremony of the Beijing Olympics. He also stood beside Hu Jintao during <a href="/wiki/60th_anniversary_of_the_founding_of_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="60th anniversary of the founding of the People&#39;s Republic of China">60th anniversary of the founding of the People's Republic of China</a> mass parade in October 2009.
</p><p>Beginning in July 2011, false reports of Jiang's death began circulating on the news media outside of mainland China and on the internet.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> While Jiang may indeed have been ill and receiving treatment, the rumours were denied by official sources.<sup id="cite_ref-time_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> On 9 October 2011, Jiang made his first public appearance since his premature obituary in Beijing at a celebration to commemorate the 100th anniversary of the <a href="/wiki/Xinhai_Revolution" title="Xinhai Revolution">Xinhai Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Jiang reappeared at the <a href="/wiki/18th_National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="18th National Congress of the Communist Party of China">18th Party Congress</a> in October 2012, and took part in the 65th Anniversary banquet of the founding of the People's Republic of China in October 2014.  At the banquet he sat next to <a href="/wiki/Xi_Jinping" title="Xi Jinping">Xi Jinping</a>, who had then succeeded Hu Jintao as party <a href="/wiki/General_Secretary_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="General Secretary of the Communist Party of China">General Secretary</a>.  In September 2015, Jiang attended the <a href="/wiki/2015_China_Victory_Day_Parade" title="2015 China Victory Day Parade">parade celebrating 70 years since end of World War II</a>; there, Jiang again sat next to Xi Jinping and Hu Jintao.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> He appeared on 29 May 2017 at Shanghai Technology University.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Xi Jinping assumed power, Jiang's position in the protocol sequence of leaders retreated; while he was often seated next to Xi Jinping at official events, his name was often reported after all standing members of the Communist Party's Politburo.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Jiang reappeared at the <a href="/wiki/19th_National_Congress_of_the_Communist_Party_of_China" title="19th National Congress of the Communist Party of China">19th Party Congress</a> on 18 October 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yangzhou_-_Wenchang_Square_-_Jiang_Zemin_inscription_-_P1070047.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Yangzhou_-_Wenchang_Square_-_Jiang_Zemin_inscription_-_P1070047.JPG/220px-Yangzhou_-_Wenchang_Square_-_Jiang_Zemin_inscription_-_P1070047.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Yangzhou_-_Wenchang_Square_-_Jiang_Zemin_inscription_-_P1070047.JPG/330px-Yangzhou_-_Wenchang_Square_-_Jiang_Zemin_inscription_-_P1070047.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Yangzhou_-_Wenchang_Square_-_Jiang_Zemin_inscription_-_P1070047.JPG/440px-Yangzhou_-_Wenchang_Square_-_Jiang_Zemin_inscription_-_P1070047.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yangzhou_-_Wenchang_Square_-_Jiang_Zemin_inscription_-_P1070047.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jiang Zemin's inscription engraved on a stone in his hometown, <a href="/wiki/Yangzhou" title="Yangzhou">Yangzhou</a></div></div></div>
<p>Jiang's legacy is subject to the debate of historians and biographers. Jiang has come under quiet criticism from within the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_China" title="Communist Party of China">Communist Party of China</a> for focusing on economic growth at all costs while ignoring the resulting environmental damage of the growth, the widening gap between rich and poor in China and the social costs absorbed by those whom economic reform has left behind.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, the policies of his successors, Hu Jintao and <a href="/wiki/Wen_Jiabao" title="Wen Jiabao">Wen Jiabao</a>, have widely been seen as efforts to address these imbalances and move away from a sole focus on economic growth toward a broader view of development which incorporates non-economic factors such as health and the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Domestically, Jiang's legacy and reputation is mixed. While some<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> people attributed the period of relative stability and growth in the 1990s to Jiang's term, others argue that Jiang did little to correct systemic imbalance and an accumulation of problems which resulted from years of breakneck-pace economic reforms, leaving the next administration facing innumerable challenges, some of which may have been too late to solve.<sup id="cite_ref-tairenxing_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tairenxing-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fact that Jiang rose to power as the direct beneficiary of the political aftermath of Tiananmen has shaped the perception of Jiang in the eyes of many. Following the Tiananmen protests, Jiang threw his support behind elder <a href="/wiki/Chen_Yun" title="Chen Yun">Chen Yun</a>'s conservative economic policies, but subsequently changed his allegiance to Deng Xiaoping's reform-oriented agenda following the latter's "Southern Tour". This shift was not only seen as the exercise of a political opportunist, it also sowed confusion among party loyalists in regards to what direction the party was headed or what the party truly believed in.<sup id="cite_ref-miles_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-miles-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> While continued economic reforms resulted in an explosion of wealth around the country, it also led to the formation of special interest groups in many sectors of the economy, and the exercise of state power without any meaningful oversight. This opened the way for the sub-optimal distribution of the fruits of growth, and an expanding culture of corruption among bureaucrats and party officials.<sup id="cite_ref-tairenxing_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tairenxing-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historian and former Xinhua journalist <a href="/wiki/Yang_Jisheng_(historian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Yang Jisheng (historian)">Yang Jisheng</a> wrote that Jiang may well have been given a positive historical assessment had it not been for his decision to 'overstay his welcome' by remaining in the Central Military Commission post after Hu had formally assumed the party leadership.  Moreover, Jiang took credit for all the gains made during the 13 years "between 1989 and 2002," which not only evoked the memories of Jiang being a beneficiary of Tiananmen, but also neglected the economic foundations laid by Deng, whose authority was still paramount until the mid-1990s.  Additionally, Jiang was also criticized for his insistence on writing the "Three Represents" into the party and state constitutions (see below), which Yang called Jiang's attempt at "self-deification", i.e., that he saw himself as a visionary along the same lines as Deng and Mao.  Yang contended, "The 'Three Represents' is just common sense. It is not a proper theoretical framework. It's what any ruler would tell the people to justify the continued rule of the governing party."<sup id="cite_ref-jisheng_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jisheng-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Three_Represents.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Three_Represents&quot;">"Three Represents"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: &quot;Three Represents&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Formally, Jiang's theory of "<a href="/wiki/Three_Represents" title="Three Represents">Three Represents</a>" was enshrined in both Party and State constitutions as an "important thought," following in the footsteps of <a href="/wiki/Marxism-Leninism" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxism-Leninism">Marxism-Leninism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong_Thought" class="mw-redirect" title="Mao Zedong Thought">Mao Zedong Thought</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deng_Xiaoping_Theory" title="Deng Xiaoping Theory">Deng Xiaoping Theory</a>. However, the theory lacked staying power. By the time of the 17th Party Congress in 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_Outlook_on_Development" title="Scientific Outlook on Development">Scientific Outlook on Development</a> had already been written into the constitution of the Communist Party, a mere five years after the Three Represents, overtaking the latter as the guiding ideology for much of <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jintao" title="Hu Jintao">Hu Jintao</a>'s term. While his successors paid lip service to "Three Represents" in official party documentation and speeches, no special emphasis was placed on the theory after Jiang left office. There was even speculation following Xi Jinping's assumption of power in 2012 that the Three Represents would eventually be dropped from the party's list of guiding ideologies.<sup id="cite_ref-datong_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-datong-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Three Represents justified the incorporation of the new capitalist business class into the party, and changed the founding ideology of the Communist Party of China from protecting the interests of the peasantry and workers to that of the "overwhelming majority of the people", a euphemism aimed at placating the growing entrepreneurial class. Conservative critics within the party, such as hardline leftist <a href="/wiki/Deng_Liqun" title="Deng Liqun">Deng Liqun</a>, denounced this as betrayal of "true" communist ideology.<sup id="cite_ref-datong_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-datong-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_areas">Other areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some have also associated Jiang with the widespread corruption and cronyism that had become a notable feature of the Communist power apparatus since Jiang's years in power. In the military, the two vice-chairmen who sat atop the Central Military Commission hierarchy &#8211; nominally as assistants to then Chairman <a href="/wiki/Hu_Jintao" title="Hu Jintao">Hu Jintao</a> &#8211; Vice-Chairmen <a href="/wiki/Xu_Caihou" title="Xu Caihou">Xu Caihou</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guo_Boxiong" title="Guo Boxiong">Guo Boxiong</a>, were said to have obstructed Hu Jintao's exercise of power in the military.  Xu and Guo were characterized as "Jiang's proxies in the military."  Eventually, both men were reported to have taken massive bribes, and both fell under the axe of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-corruption_campaign_under_Xi_Jinping" title="Anti-corruption campaign under Xi Jinping">Anti-corruption campaign under Xi Jinping</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-weakgrip_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weakgrip-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jiang's time in office also saw a notable increase of collusion between business and political elites.  A lack of checks and balances in the cadre promotion system also meant that personal loyalty often trumped skill and merit in ensuring advancement.  Many people in top ranks of the military and political elites were seen to have gotten to their high positions through securing the patronage of Jiang.  Prominent examples often cited include Jiang's former secretary <a href="/wiki/Jia_Ting%27an" title="Jia Ting&#39;an">Jia Ting'an</a> and Shanghai clique member <a href="/wiki/Huang_Ju" title="Huang Ju">Huang Ju</a>.
</p><p>At the same time, many biographers of Jiang have noted his government resembled an oligarchy as opposed to an autocratic dictatorship.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Many of the policies of his era had been attributed to others in government, notably Premier <a href="/wiki/Zhu_Rongji" title="Zhu Rongji">Zhu Rongji</a>.  Jiang was also characterized as a leader who was mindful to seek the opinion of his close advisers.  Jiang is often credited with the improvement in foreign relations during his term,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> but at the same time many Chinese have criticized him for being too conciliatory towards the United States and Russia. The issue of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_reunification" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese reunification">Chinese reunification</a> between the mainland and Taiwan gained ground during Jiang's term,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> but more substantial talks regarding Cross-Strait talks and the eventual <a href="/wiki/Three_Links" title="Three Links">Three Links</a> occurred during the term of Hu Jintao. The construction of the <a href="/wiki/Qinghai%E2%80%93Tibet_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Qinghai–Tibet Railway">Qinghai–Tibet Railway</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Three_Gorges_Dam" title="Three Gorges Dam">Three Gorges Dam</a> began under Jiang.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_reading">Further reading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jiang_Zemin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Further reading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Gilley, Bruce. "Tiger on the Brink: Jiang Zemin and China's New Elite." Berkeley: University of California Press, 1998. 395pp. This was the first biography of Jiang to appear in the West. A comprehensive and highly readable journalistic account of Jiang's early years, his ascendancy within the Party bureaucracy, and his ultimate rise to power as Deng Xiaoping's successor in the wake of Tiananmen.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robert_Lawrence_Kuhn" title="Robert Lawrence Kuhn">Kuhn, Robert Lawrence</a> = <i>The Man Who Changed China: The Life and Legacy of Jiang Zemin</i>, Random House (English edition) 2005. Century Publishing Group, Shanghai (Chinese edition) 2005. The book is a general biography of Jiang with a more favorable stance towards him.</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.chinadaily.com.cn/english/doc/2005-03/02/content_420886.htm"><i>China Daily</i></a>, chinadaily.com.cn; accessed 19 June 2015.</li>
